How the learning works

How did the points turn into a strategy?

When you played, nobody told you spikes were bad. You felt it: you stepped on a 🔺, the score dropped, and you thought “okay — don't do that again.” You hit a 💎, the score jumped, and you thought “more of that.” The reward was your only teacher. The agent learns the exact same way — let's break it down.

1

It keeps a “goodness” score for every move in every situation

Think of a giant notebook. For each thing it can see, it jots down how good each of the four moves has turned out: “spike on my right → going right has been very bad.” That number is the move's Q-value — its expected payoff. At the start, the notebook is blank: every move looks equally fine.

2

Every reward nudges the notebook

Take a move, see what happens. Land on a spike? The score for that move in that situation gets nudged down. Reach the flag? The moves that led there get nudged up. Do this thousands of times and the good moves float to the top — the same “don't do that again / more of that” you felt, just written as numbers.

3

Early on it explores; later it exploits

At first the notebook is useless, so guessing randomly is actually smart — that's how it discovers what's out there (you did this too, wandering on round 1). As the notebook fills in, it trusts it more: it stops guessing and starts picking the highest-scoring move — being greedy. Drag the timeline to watch training unfold:
